Pet Food but Human Spending

Pets are becoming more popular than ever, while the cost of owning a pet is increasing. Inspired by the webpage Economic signs that pet food may be in era of cost challenges by Tim Wall, I decided to make a further study of the data. From the page, we can find the source data from Federal Reserve Bank of St. Louis.

There are two main aspects we are learning about: the Consumer Price Index (CPI) and the Producer Price Index (PPI). As described in the article, both measure different points in the value chain, and the gap between them helps illustrate margin dynamics, cost recovery, and pricing strategies across the industry. CPI represents pet food costs for consumers, including pet food in general rather than only cat and dog food. PPI represents the cost of production for pet food manufacturers.

These indices use a base year where the index equals 100. Each later value shows the percentage change from that base year. For example, if the index is 150, the cost has increased by 50% since the base year.

The original page focused on the relationship between CPI and PPI for dog food in particular. That raised another question for me: what about cat food? Is cat PPI different from dog PPI, and does comparing both give a better picture of pet food costs? The following study explores those questions.

I start with a general comparison between CPI and PPI with both cat and dog food. From the graph, we can see that they generally follow the same overall trend, which suggests that production cost is closely related to the final cost faced by consumers. This study uses data from 2002 to 2024, which covers a longer period than the original article.

Which Type of Food Has a Higher Inflation Rate?

Which food is facing a faster-growing cost over time? To answer this question, I calculated the cumulative change in PPI for cat food and dog food. The change is using the average value of PPI in a year to compare with the base year's average. In our case, the base year is 2002. The cumulative change is then considered as a factor to show how fast the prices of cat food and dog food were growing.

Chart 5 Formula

Cumulative Change in Year y = Δ1 + Δ2 + ... + Δy

Here, Δi represents the yearly change in index value for year i.

C(y) = ∑i=1y Δi

For example, if the yearly changes are +3 in 2003 and +5 in 2004, then the cumulative value in 2004 is 8 rather than just 5.
